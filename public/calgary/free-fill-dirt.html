Why free fill is available in Calgary

Calgary's clean-fill tipping rate is cheap on paper ($0–$10/tonne), but the round-trip costs aren't. Trucking time, fuel, scale-house waits, and (for commercial loads) Calgary's clean-fill approval process all add up. For sites with even a hint of sod, mixed debris, or contamination, the tipping itself jumps to $113–$180/tonne. Either way, putting the material in a local taker's hands is the cheaper, faster path. That's why listings on Dirtlink are real — the supply side has a real reason to give it away.

What's typically available

  • Site-stripped soil from basement and foundation digs across Calgary
  • Clean clay fill from grading projects
  • Surplus topsoil from finished landscape jobs (less common but high quality)
  • Mixed fill from utility trench backfilling (verify quality before taking)

Watch for these before accepting free fill

  • Contamination — confirm no asphalt, concrete, organics, or buried debris.
  • Soil type — Calgary "gumbo" clay holds water and can be hard to work with for landscaping.
  • Loading equipment — large jobs may have a loader on site; small jobs may need you to bring one.
  • Access — confirm truck access to the load point.

Frequently asked questions

Is free fill dirt actually free?
Yes — but you typically arrange and pay for hauling. The supplier saves the tipping fee; you save the purchase cost.
Where does free Calgary fill come from?
Mostly from active excavation, basement, and foundation projects. Excavators pay to dump fill at landfills, so giving it away is a savings for them.
How fast do free fill listings move?
Often within hours during peak season. Set up alerts on Dirtlink to be notified when matching listings post.
Is free fill safe for raised garden beds?
No — free fill is structural material. For gardens, use screened topsoil or a loam blend.
Do I need a permit to haul free fill in Calgary?
For homeowner-scale loads in a personal pickup or small dump trailer, no. For commercial hauling, standard hauler regulations apply.
